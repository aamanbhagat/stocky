A **balance sheet** is a snapshot of what a company owns and owes on a given date. For investors researching NSE and BSE listed companies, it is the fastest way to judge financial health before looking at the share price.

## The core equation

Every balance sheet follows one rule:

**Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity**

- **Assets** — what the company owns (cash, inventory, plant, investments).
- **Liabilities** — what it owes (loans, payables).
- **Equity** — the owners' residual stake.

## Ratios that matter

### Debt-to-equity
Total borrowings divided by equity. A lower figure generally means less financial risk, though "good" varies by sector.

### Current ratio
Current assets divided by current liabilities. Above 1 suggests the company can cover its short-term dues.

## What to watch over time

Look at three to five years, not one. Rising equity, controlled debt and steady current assets point to a durable business.

## Frequently asked questions

### Where do I find a company's balance sheet?
In its annual report, on the NSE/BSE filing pages, or on financial data aggregators.

### Is a high debt always bad?
Not necessarily. Capital-heavy sectors like infrastructure carry more debt by nature; compare within the same industry.

### How is a balance sheet different from a P&L?
A balance sheet is a point-in-time snapshot; a profit and loss statement covers performance over a period.
